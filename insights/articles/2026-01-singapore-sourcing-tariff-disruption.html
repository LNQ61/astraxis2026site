Trade Disruption Is Reshaping How Singapore Pharma Sites Think About Supplier Access

US tariffs, China supply chain pressure, and a flight to stable sourcing — what it means for procurement teams in Singapore.

Astraxis International

The US-China trade conflict that escalated through 2025 created material pressure on global pharmaceutical supply chains. US tariffs on Chinese pharmaceutical imports, and China's retaliatory measures, disrupted established sourcing patterns — particularly for APIs and specialised chemicals. Singapore, as a major re-exporter and manufacturing hub, was not insulated from the effect: non-oil domestic exports fell 11.3 percent in August 2025, with US-bound shipments down nearly 29 percent. The disruption was broad enough that procurement teams at Singapore pharma and biotech sites were revisiting their supplier maps.

Singapore pharma firms sought clarity on US tariff exemptions through Q3 and Q4 2025. The picture that emerged was mixed: API manufacturers faced limited direct impact under most tariff schedules, while exporters of finished branded products faced greater exposure. The 100-percent tariff on branded pharmaceuticals announced by the US in late 2025 introduced additional uncertainty for companies with US distribution exposure. For Singapore-based procurement teams, the practical effect was a sharper focus on supply chain resilience: who are the backup suppliers, what is their documentation status, and how quickly can they respond when the primary route is under pressure?

Despite this disruption, EDB announced in February 2026 that Singapore attracted S$14.2 billion in fixed asset investment commitments in 2025 — with biomedical manufacturers contributing a significant share, responding to rising demand for high-value biopharma and medtech products. Singapore's fundamentals — regulatory clarity, world-class infrastructure, and the Tuas biomedical cluster — remained intact as a sourcing and manufacturing base. The message from the investment data is consistent: disruption at the trade level does not change Singapore's structural position, but it does change how procurement teams manage their supplier relationships within it.

The companies that navigated 2025 best were those with documented, responsive supplier relationships already in place — not those who started looking for alternatives when disruption hit. Qualification processes that had been treated as optional or deferred became urgent. Suppliers who already had ISO records, CoAs, and a track record of prompt communication were prioritised over lower-cost alternatives that could not respond at pace.

Why it matters to Astraxis

The trade disruption of 2025 reinforced what experienced procurement teams already know — supplier relationships built on documentation, accountability, and local presence hold up better under pressure than those built purely on price and convenience. Regional sourcing through a coordinator with Singapore healthcare context reduces risk when global supply chains move unpredictably.
